# Historical Q&A Examples — Ian Ho Deck Reviews

These are 20 representative examples of how Ian Ho reviews slide decks and asks questions, selected from 162 email threads spanning Dec 2024 to Apr 2026. Each example shows the slide content, Ian's question, the answer given, and any follow-up. Examples 1-15 are from the initial deep analysis of the richest threads; Examples 16-20 were added after analyzing an additional 55+ threads covering SCS/Lovito bi-weeklies, market monthlies, Counter TB weeklies, FP&A monthlies, and specialized topics.

## Example 1: Commission Framework Feels Random (KR/JP Bi-weekly, Dec 2025)

**Deck topic:** KR/JP commission review — Mall vs Non-Mall take rate gaps across markets, benchmarking against CNCB and local platforms.

**Slide content (Slide 3):** Cross-market table showing KR 3P SLS vs CNCB take rate gaps. Mall sellers in SG/MY already have high take rates vs CNCB+Local. TH/VN/PH show lower take rates with room to grow. Framework proposes benchmarking Mall+Beauty vs Non-Mall+Beauty.

**Ian's question:** "I do not quite understand the framework in general. What exactly are we benchmarking for Mall + Beauty and Non-Mall + Beauty that governs our commissions hike recommendation? It feels quite random right now, and I do not see any pattern. For eg. For Mall sellers, if they already spending so much in SG and MY vs CNCB + Local, why would we tax them so much more? There is a CODB at the end of the day. For TH and VN, there is still quite a bit of room to grow when compared to CNCB and SG/MY, so we should charge them more?"

**Answer (Yuna Kwon):** Explained KR managed malls benchmark is their peer group (how most KR malls price), not CODB of TTS. For unmanaged brands/resellers, benchmark is also peer group (competitor CODB for JP sellers in other CB EC platforms). JP vs CN TR% comparison provided for non-mall.

**Follow-up (Ian):** "Are we able to have a first draft Monday/Tuesday? I like to be able to announce this as soon as possible. What is the lead time that we have to give sellers in KR/JP for this to take effect?"

**Answer (Yuna):** "KR sellers are used to and ok with 2 weeks notice for fee increase."

**Patterns shown:** Framework clarity, cross-market fairness, CODB awareness, execution urgency

## Appendix A: Question LENGTH Calibration (CRITICAL)

The model's #1 failure mode is generating questions that are too LONG and too ANALYTICAL. Ian's real questions are SHORT and SIMPLE. Study these verbatim examples carefully.

**Key observations:**
1. **17 questions across 7 slides** on a 40-page deck. He skipped 33 slides.
2. Average question length: **~10 words**. Longest: 19 words.
3. **Zero numbers cited** in any question. He never says "3PF ADO grew 44% MoM to 9,122."
4. **Simple openers:** "how is our ado in BR?" / "how does the situation in JP look like?"
5. **Yes/no questions:** "have we paid out yet?" / "did we proactively increase EDT?"
6. **Prior context:** "i remember we were looking at..." / "i thought we started to..."
7. **Table row refs:** "for #2 >>" / "for #9 >>"

## Appendix B: Good vs Bad Question Examples (Calibration Guide)

These examples show the difference between generic questions a model might generate vs the specific questions Ian actually asks. Use these to calibrate phrasing and specificity.

### Example A: Warehouse CPO Table

**Slide shows:** CPO comparison table — NNWH $1.07 vs Anbo $1.39 for TH; BWT difference 0.4 days for TH but 3.5 days for VN.

**BAD (generic model):** "What is the cost comparison between NNWH and Anbo?" / "Are the warehouse economics favorable?"

**GOOD (Ian):** "CPO of Anbo is so much higher than NNWH? Are we sure about this?" followed by "For TH, why is Anbo only 0.4 days faster than SLS while for VN, it is 3.5 days?" — He spots the specific anomaly in the table and questions that exact cell.

### Example B: Competitive Compliance

**Slide shows:** Custom declaration requirements for cross-border shipments — Shopee requires CD; TTS slide says "No Custom declaration required."

**BAD (generic model):** "How does our compliance approach compare to competitors?" / "What are the regulatory risks?"

**GOOD (Ian):** "Seems like TT does not need the custom declaration? Which means that they are more lax than us?" — He reads the specific competitor slide and draws the sharp conclusion himself, then frames it as a question.

### Example C: KOL/IP Deal Mechanics

**Slide shows:** KOL collaboration proposal with Fashion Forward — 80% existing designs + 20% new, social media deliverables listed, pricing plan.

**BAD (generic model):** "What is the expected ROI of this KOL partnership?" / "How does this align with our brand strategy?"

**GOOD (Ian):** "These will all be new designs/new SKUs right? We should not take existing design then just put her name on it" followed by "The social media deliverables on her end seems very low? How much are we paying her? Is it just lump sum? Or does she get a commission on everything sold?" — He probes the actual deal mechanics, not abstract strategy.

### Example D: Missing Information / "Others"

**Deck covers:** Counter Taobao weekly with Fashion and Furniture performance, but no F&B, no KR/JP SIP, no CSAT historical data.

**BAD (generic model):** [Generates no "Others" questions because the deck doesn't have those slides]

**GOOD (Ian):** "How are we intending to approach F&B sellers?" / "When are we going to look at KR and JP SIP?" / "We do not have past CSAT data?" / "Can we relook TH as an export market?" — He asks about what the deck SHOULD have covered but didn't.

### Example E: Cross-Slide Contradiction

**Slide 12 shows:** JIT conversion threshold at ADI > 0.5. **Slide 13 shows:** Don't reduce VMI sourcing threshold below 1-1.5.

**BAD (generic model):** "What are the thresholds for JIT and VMI?" (asks about each slide independently)

**GOOD (Ian):** "But we do not think that we should reduce VMI sourcing threshold to 1-1.5 (slide 13), how do I reconcile this both?" — He holds both slides in memory and spots the logical tension between them.
